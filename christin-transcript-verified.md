# Christin Morse — Documentary Transcript
### Manna Bakery | Verified Source Quotes

## On the Pattern of God's Provision

> "When I look back I see a pattern of Mana in my life from the beginning. God was just working the story in my heart just one day at a time, one piece of bread at a time, so that when my eyes were open I was able to receive the Full Gospel."

> "God was just working this story in my heart just one day at a time, one piece of bread at a time, so that when it was ready and I was — my eyes were open — I was able to receive the Full Gospel and understand it."

---

## On Manna & Daily Trust

> "They had to learn to trust him one day at a time and gather just enough for one day. Just one day. One day at a time we can just trust God with what we need for today and not worry about tomorrow because he's enough for us day by day by day. Just trust me today. I'm enough. I'm enough for today. I'm enough for you."

> "The Israelites — they didn't do anything or earn their Mana. He didn't say I'm going to send Mana if you're keeping the Commandments. He sent it every day no matter what they did. It was a tangible witness to his love for them. 'I'm here every day, I'm going to provide, I'll be here.' Just accept it and receive it — enjoy it."

> "The Mana from Heaven, just one day at a time, one piece of bread at a time."

---

## On Naming the Bakery

> "I named my business Mana, Bread from Heaven, based on that moment when I was reading the scriptures and Jesus showed me — I am the bread, I am the life — and you can eat bread and satisfy your stomach for a little while but I will satisfy you long term."

> "For the very little bit of time and money that I had, it was incredible how Mana was able to come together. Like bread from Heaven, just one day at a time. You need this — here it is. If you need this — here it is."

---

## On Surrender & New Beginnings

> "It actually — I wouldn't describe it as courage. I would describe it more as surrender. It was more of a letting go."

> "I was putting this all on me, all on my plate. I was wanting to control the outcome because I didn't know there was any other way. I thought this is my life, this is my marriage, this is my town and my children and so I have a responsibility for this to turn out good — and I just knew I wasn't able to do it anymore."

> "In that moment I just was able to breathe for the first time."

> "I felt like I had stepped from a dark long winter. I had just come from a very cold snowy season of my life and on this day I felt like the spring was coming and some hope was coming back into my heart."

---

## On What the Gospel Really Is

> "It's been really humbling as I realize how much he's gone out of his way — just for me to know him and to know his love. And to know that it's not through the steps and the ordinances and the attendance roles and the visiting teaching — all of that stuff was for not. That's not what the whole point is. The whole point is just Jesus. And that him reaching me, rather than me having to strive for him — that was what the gospel is all about. So simple."

> "It's just Jesus. And I know that sounds simple right now but when you get here — when you make that switch and you start swimming in all of his love and his abundance and his grace — you'll get it. You'll know what I'm saying. It's just Jesus. We don't need to add to Jesus. He's plenty. He's enough. He's not only enough — he's everything."

> "One never leaves the one behind. Jesus is everything."

---

## On the Bakery as Gathering

> "I love how people pause when they're eating bread. It's a time where they just rest. And that's what I see when people are going and taking his body and his blood that he shed for us."

> "I come early. I like to slice it and cube it and then I'll slowly walk around all the stations and I get to enjoy that worship — almost like an early private moment, just with me and them. And I'm able to worship God while I'm walking around and putting all the bread in the bowls. It's really special."

---

## On Community & Belonging

> "I stepped up to the water and I looked out to my new family and I realized that I had a new home."

> "It's God, you know. And it's the family of Christ and the Believers."

---

## On the Manna Story Coming Together

> "God provided the right people — bringing someone into my life that would just start wrecking all my thoughts on Mormonism, seeing the people in Kenya the way they were living for God — just everything at the right time."

> "He's still providing daily — for me, for my family, and for the people here."

---

## On Resilience & Hope

> "I have a resilient sense of hope — like a beach ball in a swimming pool. I felt like everything was somehow going to be okay, over and over again."

> "I saw this good man who had these vulnerabilities and temptations — don't we all? I've got my vulnerabilities."

## Background Context (Not for Direct Use — Internal Reference Only)

- Christin was raised in a strict vegetarian Mormon household in Monticello, Utah
- She married young and had six children: Christa, D, Joseph, Jeffrey, Benjamin, and Shaden
- Her marriage of 19+ years ended after her husband's addiction led to a criminal confession; she initiated the separation
- She received an unexpected $90,000 investment from a near-stranger to launch the bakery
- She found the bakery's first location by presenting hot bread at a lease negotiation — no financial backing
- Friends and volunteers appeared organically for staffing — "people came to me, I didn't even have to put in the paper"
- She traveled to Kenya multiple times to visit a Facebook connection named Kia, whose village of 10,000 Protestant Christians profoundly reshaped her theology
- A man she met through an online dating app (an Evangelical Christian from the East Coast) helped her work through her theological transition out of Mormonism
- Her children have each taken different faith paths; several have also left Mormonism
- The Narnia scene (canning peaches, listening to the movie) was the pivotal moment of her surrender and born-again experience
- The name "Manna" came from a quiet morning reading scripture on a porch in Africa, just before receiving devastating news about her husband
